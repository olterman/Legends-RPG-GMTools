# The Human Tribes
Among the elder races, humans are often dismissed as brief sparks—short-lived, impulsive, and fragile. Yet it is precisely this impermanence that has shaped humanity into the most adaptable and unpredictable of all peoples. Where immortals plan in centuries, humans act in generations, and in doing so they have carved nations, faiths, and empires across the Lands of Legends.
Though countless minor cultures exist, most scholars divide humanity into several major tribal and cultural groupings, shaped by geography, belief, and ancient catastrophe.
---
## The Caldorans
The **Caldorans** are the architects of the greatest human empire the world has yet seen. Their power radiates outward from the city of **Caldor**, a marvel of stone, canals, workshops, and towering academies. From this beating heart, Caldor’s influence spreads through roads, trade routes, and treaties rather than conquest alone.
Caldoran culture is defined by **innovation, commerce, and intellectual openness**. They stand at the dawn of a human renaissance—rediscovering ancient knowledge, experimenting with new technologies, and questioning traditions long held sacred. Art, engineering, natural philosophy, and proto-scientific inquiry flourish side by side.
The Caldorans believe that progress is not only inevitable, but _moral_. To improve the world is seen as a sacred duty, even if that improvement unsettles older powers.
Key traits of Caldorans include:
- Cosmopolitan cities filled with diverse peoples
- Powerful merchant guilds and trade consortiums
- A growing middle class of artisans, scholars, and explorers
- Political intrigue masked behind civility and law
To allies, the Caldorans are enlightened partners. To rivals, they are dangerous idealists whose ambition may reshape the world faster than it can endure.
---
## The Xanthir
Far to the south lies the harsh dominion of the **Xanthir**, a militarized theocracy devoted wholly to **The Red God**. Their lands are hemmed in by wild frontiers—jungles, deserts, and monster-haunted ruins—which they interpret as divine trials rather than obstacles.
Xanthir society is built upon absolute faith and rigid discipline. Every citizen is a servant of the god-state, and every action—war, labor, prayer—is considered a form of worship. The priesthood and military are inseparable; generals are clerics, and temples double as fortresses.
The Red God is associated with blood, sacrifice, purification through fire, and righteous conquest. Outsiders often mistake Xanthir zeal for madness, but their campaigns are calculated, methodical, and terrifyingly effective.
Xanthir culture is marked by:
- Strict caste roles ordained by religious law
- Mandatory military service
- Ritual scarification and blood rites
- Monumental temples built atop conquered lands
To the Xanthir, mercy is weakness, doubt is heresy, and survival is proof of divine favor.

## The Fenmir
The **Fenmir** are considered the oldest of the human peoples. Their name derives from the Elvish _finir_—_the mortals_—a term once used dismissively by the elder races, later embraced by humans as a mark of pride. Long ago, the Fenmir formed the first great human settlements of the known world, but invasion, catastrophe, and internal fracture shattered that early unity.
Over generations, Fenmir culture adapted to harsh uplands, contested borders, and spiritual necessity. What outsiders once separated into different names and labels—_Highlanders_, _uplanders_, _spirit-walkers_—are in truth a **single people**, bound by shared ancestry, belief, and tradition.
The Fenmir are a **nomadic and semi-nomadic tribal culture**, deeply spiritual and fiercely proud. They revere ancestors, land-spirits, and the cyclical nature of life and death. Their shamans serve as healers, judges, historians, and intermediaries between the seen and unseen worlds. Ritual, song, and ordeal are central to Fenmir identity.
Physically, the Fenmir tend toward tall, powerfully built frames, light skin often marked by pale blue hues attributed to ancient bloodlines and highland climates. Their bodies are adorned with **ritual tattoos**, each earned through trials, vision-quests, or acts of honor. To damage or defile these markings is among the gravest insults known to Fenmir law.
Though capable of great brutality in war, Fenmir culture is governed by strict codes of honor. Oaths are sacred, hospitality is binding, and betrayal is remembered for generations. Among all human peoples, the Fenmir are regarded as some of the most martial and spiritually disciplined.
---
### The Highland Fenmir
The **Highland Fenmir** inhabit the rugged uplands and mountain regions. Over generations, they have grown taller and broader than their lowland kin, with pale skin often marked by light blue hues—traits attributed to climate, diet, and ancient bloodlines.
They are a **traditional, proud, and martial people**, valuing honor, lineage, and endurance. Warfare among Highland Fenmir is ritualized, governed by codes older than written law. They maintain strong oral histories and revere ancestral heroes.
Despite their severity, they are not cruel. Oaths are sacred, hospitality is binding, and betrayal is remembered for generations.
---
### The Lowland Fenmir
The **Lowland Fenmir** once thrived in fertile plains and riverlands, but were nearly annihilated during devastating Uruk incursions. Entire bloodlines vanished, cities burned, and the survivors scattered.
Those who endured fled to the coast, where they founded the **Free Cities**—independent city-states bound by mutual defense, trade, and shared bitterness. These cities became havens for:
- Refugees and exiles
- Privateers and freebooters
- Mercenaries, smugglers, and opportunists
Lowland Fenmir culture prizes **freedom, adaptability, and cunning**. Authority is questioned, alliances are fluid, and survival often depends on wit rather than strength.
### The Lowland Fenmir and the Free Cities
Not all Fenmir followed the old nomadic ways.
When Uruk incursions swept through the lowlands, entire Fenmir clans were destroyed. The survivors fled toward the coasts, where necessity forced adaptation. These refugees founded the **Free Cities**—independent coastal city-states bound by mutual defense, trade, and a shared memory of near-annihilation.
Lowland Fenmir culture prizes **freedom above all else**, tempered by hard-earned adaptability and ruthless pragmatism. In the Free Cities, authority is never assumed—it is negotiated, challenged, or earned anew with every tide. Alliances shift as quickly as the winds, and survival depends as much on silvered tongues, cunning bargains, and well-kept secrets as on steel and shot.
These coastal cities have become **true crossroads of the world**, welcoming all races, all creeds, and all outcasts willing to live by the laws of the sea. Exiles, heretics, escaped slaves, foreign merchants, and elder-folk walk the same docks. From this mingling has emerged a culture unlike any other: a fierce brotherhood of **privateers, freebooters, smugglers, and mercenary captains**, bound not by blood or banner, but by shared risk and mutual profit.
The sea is their true sovereign. Its moods shape their customs, its dangers their superstitions, and its bounty their wealth. Ships are treated as kin, ports as temporary havens, and loyalty is measured in deeds rather than lineage.
Yet beneath this apparent lawlessness lies an older order. Even the most irreverent Free City denizen respects the authority of **Fenmir shamans and ancestral law** in matters that transcend coin and contract. Oath-breaking, kin-slaughter, and spiritual transgression are judged not by captains or councils, but by traditions far older than the harbors themselves—reminding all who dwell there that freedom without memory is merely chaos.

## Scattered Human Peoples
Beyond the great powers and named tribes exist countless **scattered human settlements**, often overlooked by scholars but vital to the living fabric of the world.
In the **Xanthir Wetlands**, isolated human communities endure under the shadow of the Red God’s theocracy. Some are tolerated vassals who provide food, labor, or military service. Others are hidden enclaves of heretics, escaped slaves, or forgotten tribes who survive among swamps, mangroves, and ruin-choked waterways. These wetland peoples are hardened, insular, and deeply suspicious of outsiders, relying on local spirits, half-forgotten gods, and practical magic to endure.
Far to the north and east, along the shifting dunes and blasted plateaus known as **The Sands**, small human tribes cling to existence against impossible odds. Some are descendants of ancient caravan cities swallowed by desert. Others claim lineage from peoples who fled cataclysms long forgotten. Life here is harsh and often short, and customs tend toward fatalism, ancestor reverence, and ruthless pragmatism.
Many of these scattered peoples serve as intermediaries, smugglers, guides, or unwilling pawns in greater struggles between empires, gods, and elder races. Though few in number, their knowledge of forbidden routes, hidden ruins, and ancient truths makes them far more significant than their size suggests.
---
## A Fragmented Legacy
Though divided by empire, faith, and survival, all human tribes share a common truth: their time is short, and they know it. This knowledge drives them to build, conquer, believe, and defy fate itself.
Where elder races endure, **humanity strives**—and in that striving lies both their greatest strength and their greatest danger.
