# The Small Folk
_Those Who Walk Beneath Notice_
The peoples collectively known as the **Small Folk** are often dismissed by larger races as minor, harmless, or quaint. This belief has proven disastrous more than once.
Though diminutive in stature, the Small Folk are **ancient, clever, and deeply entangled with the hidden workings of the world**. They thrive in places others overlook: deep forests, forgotten tunnels, broken machines, and the seams between realities. Their small size is not a weakness, but a survival strategy—allowing them to persist through ages of upheaval unnoticed, underestimated, and unchallenged.
---
## The Gitz
_The Clever Tribes of the Wild_
The **Gitz** are a nimble, sharp-witted people who dwell primarily in the **Fenmir wilds**, favoring dense forests, rugged hills, and tangled borderlands where larger civilizations struggle to maintain control.
Though often mistaken for feral scavengers or degenerate Uruk kin, the Gitz possess **keen intellects, sophisticated tribal cultures, and remarkable ingenuity**. Their societies are organized around extended clans, each defined by specialized skills: trap-making, herbal alchemy, animal handling, stealth warfare, or cunning diplomacy.
Physically small and quick, Gitz compensate for their size with:
- Ingenious mechanical traps and ambushes
- Deep knowledge of terrain and ecology
- Coordinated pack tactics
- A tradition of improvisational problem-solving
Their resemblance to the Uruk is a source of persistent rumor and prejudice. While some scholars speculate on a distant, ancient connection, the Gitz themselves reject such claims fiercely. To them, survival through wit is a mark of superiority over brute strength.
Despite their reputation, Gitz are capable traders and allies—provided they are treated with respect and caution. Insult a Gitz tribe, and you may never see the retaliation coming.
---
## The Vaettyr
_The Burrowed Tinker-Folk_
The **Vaettyr** are a highly intelligent, subterranean people found beneath nearly every major landmass. Rarely encountered in large numbers, they live in **burrows, vaults, and forgotten tunnels**, often directly beneath the feet of those who do not know they exist.
Vaettyr culture revolves around **curiosity, invention, and careful experimentation**. They are master tinkerers, engineers, and alchemists, renowned for their intricate devices, clockworks, and unusual mechanisms that blend practical engineering with esoteric principles.
Key traits of Vaettyr society include:
- Communal workshops instead of temples
- Knowledge passed through apprenticeships and schematic-songs
- An obsession with improving even functional designs
- A cautious but relentless pursuit of innovation
Vaettyr rarely seek dominance or conquest. Instead, they insert themselves quietly into existing structures, repairing, modifying, and occasionally sabotaging systems they deem inefficient or dangerous.
Some whisper that many Vaettyr devices are deliberately left incomplete—not from error, but from fear of what full success might unleash.
---
## The Velim
_Guardians of the Threshold_
The **Velim** are the most enigmatic of the Small Folk, and perhaps of all peoples.
They are tiny beings of **pure or near-pure magical essence**, often appearing as luminous, translucent, or softly glowing forms barely anchored to physical reality. The Velim are not born in any conventional sense, nor do they age as mortals understand it.
Their purpose is singular and absolute.
The Velim are **guardians of thresholds**—portals, ley-line crossings, dimensional fractures, and other points where reality grows thin. They perceive the multiverse as a web of doors and passages, and it is their duty to ensure that these remain balanced, sealed, or opened only when necessary.
Among their kind:
- Speech is rare; communication is often telepathic or symbolic
- Time is experienced non-linearly
- Individual identity matters less than function
- Failure is catastrophic, not personal
Velim can open portals between worlds—but doing so always carries a cost. Every opening weakens a boundary somewhere else. As such, they are deeply conservative in their actions, intervening only when reality itself is at risk.
Many planar disasters, scholars believe, began not with Velim action—but with mortals **forcing the gates they were meant to guard**.
---
## The Hidden Influence
The Small Folk rarely rule, rarely conquer, and rarely leave monuments.
Yet forests remain standing because of Gitz vigilance. Cities function because of Vaettyr maintenance no one notices. Worlds remain separate because the Velim endure.
Ignore the Small Folk at your peril.
They have survived every age by being overlooked—and they intend to survive the next.
## Ancient Ties and Uncomfortable Myths
Among the Small Folk, history is not written to glorify arrival or conquest. It is remembered as **impact**—what changed, what broke, and what had to be endured afterward.
Their myths do not agree with Alfirin chronicles.
They were never meant to.
---
## The Gitz and the First Shadowed Age
Gitz oral tradition speaks of a time _before names_, when the land itself was unstable and dangerous in ways no longer remembered. In these stories, the forests were not empty when the Alfirin arrived—but **unfinished**.
According to Gitz myth, their ancestors were already there, small, quick, and adaptable, surviving in a world where reality itself had not yet settled. They tell of a great **Sky-Fall**—a tearing of the world that brought radiant beings from beyond and reshaped the land in their wake.
The Gitz do not describe the Alfirin as gods or masters.
They describe them as **disruptors**.
In these tales:
- Forests grew wild and aggressive in response to new energies
- Beasts mutated, flourished, or went mad
- The land demanded new kinds of cunning to survive
The Gitz adapted. They learned faster. They hid better. They survived by becoming what the world required.
This stands in direct contradiction to Alfirin accounts, which describe their arrival as a gentle settlement into unclaimed lands. Gitz elders scoff at this notion.
> _“If the world was empty,”_ they ask, _“who learned it first?”_

---
## The Vaettyr and the Deep Fracture
Vaettyr myths go deeper—literally and figuratively.
They claim that when the Alfirin breached the dimensional aether, the shock did not merely alter the surface world. It **fractured the deep foundations of reality itself**: leylines, bedrock strata, and the hidden systems that keep magic stable.
The Vaettyr believe they existed _before_ this event, but in a dormant, scattered state—caretakers of the deep places with no need to act.
The arrival of the Alfirin changed that.
Their stories say:
- The deep world cracked and warped
- Magical pressure accumulated underground
- Systems meant to regulate reality began to fail
The Vaettyr awakened fully in response, organizing not as conquerors or rulers, but as **repairers**. They dug, reinforced, redirected flows, and built devices meant to stabilize what had been destabilized.
They do not accuse the Alfirin of malice.
But they reject the notion of innocence.
Vaettyr elders maintain that many of their oldest constructions predate all surface empires—including the first Alfirin cities. Alfirin scholars dispute this fiercely, insisting such works must be derivative or misunderstood.
The Vaettyr offer no counterargument.
They simply continue maintaining machines no one remembers commissioning.
## The Velim and the Alfirin
_The Gate That Should Have Closed_
Before the Alfirin came to the Lands of Legends, there were **no open roads between worlds**—only doors that listened.
The Velim were the keepers of those doors.
They did not rule the Gates, nor did they command them. They _attended_ them, sensing strain, balance, and resonance between realms. Passage was possible, but it was rare, brief, and precise. Nothing crossed without consequence.
---
## The Alfirin Petition
Velim memory says the Alfirin did not arrive unannounced.
They **asked**.
Their homeworld was dying—shattered by cataclysm, its leylines broken, its skies burning or collapsing. The Alfirin sought not conquest, but survival. They petitioned the Velim for passage into a stable world where they could endure.
This alone sets Velim tradition apart from later Alfirin chronicles, which portray the exodus as a feat of will and mastery.
According to the Velim, no Gate opens to mastery.
It opens to **accord**.
---
## The Negotiation at the Threshold
The Velim agreed—_conditionally_.
Their role was to open a **temporary corridor**, narrow and self-healing, one that would close behind the Alfirin ships once passage was complete. The Alfirin would arrive as **guests**, not anchors.
But something went wrong.
Velim accounts do not agree on what.
Some say:
- The Alfirin underestimated the scale of their migration
- Immortality and mass passage created unforeseen resonance
- Alfirin light and magic resisted closure
- Others whisper:
- A faction among the Alfirin attempted to stabilize the Gate permanently
- Fear of extinction overrode restraint
- The corridor was _reinforced_ instead of released
- What is certain is this:
The Gate did not close.
---
## The Damage
The failed passage tore stress-lines through reality:
- Leylines buckled
- Thresholds multiplied and thinned
- Magic pooled where it should have flowed
- The world accepted the Alfirin—but at a cost it still pays
The Velim were forced to change their purpose overnight.
No longer mere attendants, they became **guardians**, sealing breaches, stabilizing fractures, and standing watch where reality had been weakened.
This was not punishment.
It was containment.
---
## The Silence Between Them
Alfirin histories speak little of the Velim after the arrival.
Velim myths speak of **withdrawal**.
The negotiation was never formally concluded. No true pact was signed. The Velim did not banish the Alfirin—but they never forgave the breach.
From that age onward:
- The Gates became watched, not shared
- Passage became dangerous and rare
- The Velim ceased direct counsel with elder races
They learned that even good intentions can scar worlds.
---
## The Contradiction
Alfirin scholars insist:
- The passage was lawful and sanctioned
- Any damage was inevitable and later resolved
- The Velim emerged _after_ the Gates stabilized
Velim say nothing publicly.
But among themselves, they still mark the world’s age from that moment:
> _“From the Gate That Stayed Open.”_

---
## What This Means Now
If Velim memory is true, then:
- The world exists in a state of managed fracture
- Alfirin immortality is sustained by a wound that never healed
- The Gates remain closed not out of cruelty, but caution
- And should another people attempt such passage…
…the Velim may refuse to listen at all.
