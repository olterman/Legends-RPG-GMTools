# The Duergar
_The Deepbound, the Stone-Oath Folk_
No one remembers the first city the Duergar built on the surface.
This is not because it was destroyed, but because the Duergar themselves **abandoned memory of it**. Among their people, forgetting can be an act of survival.
Where other races spread across the lands, the Duergar went **downward**. They carved homes beneath mountains, deserts, and ancient seabeds, following veins of stone and metal as others follow rivers and roads. To them, the world above is transient—weather, politics, and empires all change too quickly to be trusted.
Stone endures. Pressure endures. So do the Duergar.
---
## Children of the Deep
Duergar are a compact, powerfully built people with dense musculature and heavy bone. Their skin ranges from ash-gray to iron-black, often veined with mineral hues or scarred by ritual etching. Their eyes reflect light faintly, like polished stone in torchglow.
They believe themselves shaped not by gods, but by **depth and weight**. Duergar children are taught early that hardship is not cruelty—it is **instruction**. Comfort is viewed with suspicion, as something that dulls the edge of survival.
They rarely laugh loudly, but when they do, it is sudden and unsettling.
---
## The Holds and the Stone-Oaths
Duergar society is organized around **Holds**—vast subterranean city-complexes that function as fortress, forge, archive, and shrine. Each Hold is governed not by a single ruler, but by a web of **Stone-Oaths**: binding agreements sworn between guilds, families, and castes.
To break a Stone-Oath is the greatest crime known to Duergar law.
Oaths are carved into living rock, inscribed in metals meant to outlast generations, and memorized by specialized oath-keepers who recite them during rites and disputes. A Duergar’s identity is defined less by lineage than by the oaths they carry.
---
## A Culture of Craft and Constraint
Creation is sacred to the Duergar—but never careless.
Duergar craftsmanship is renowned not for beauty, but for **purpose**. Every object is designed to endure pressure, heat, corrosion, and time. Ornamentation exists, but only where it reinforces structure or meaning.
Art among the Duergar takes the form of:
- Architecture shaped by resonance and load
- Metalwork tuned to specific stresses
- Rhythmic forge-chants used to synchronize labor
- Memorial reliefs recording endurance rather than victory
Innovation is permitted, even encouraged—but only after it has been tested to failure repeatedly.
---
## Memory, Silence, and the Deep Archives
The Duergar do not record history as other peoples do.
Instead, they maintain **Deep Archives**—sealed chambers where memories deemed dangerous, destabilizing, or corrosive are entombed. These may include:
- Failed Holds
- Forbidden technologies
- Names of oath-breakers
- Truths that once shattered a generation
Access to the Deep Archives requires unanimous consent from multiple guilds, and even then, the act is considered spiritually perilous.
Some Duergar believe the Archives are growing too large.
Others believe they are not sealed deeply enough.
---
## Relations with the Surface World
To surface dwellers, Duergar are often perceived as grim, secretive, and unyielding. They trade reluctantly, preferring clear contracts and physical guarantees over trust or goodwill.
They respect:
- The Fenmir, for endurance and oath-keeping
- The Alfirin, for ancient memory—though they distrust their detachment
- The Uruk, for raw strength, but consider them dangerously uncontrolled
They deeply distrust the Lilim.
Not because the Lilim are dangerous—but because they **do not stay**. To the Duergar, motion without anchor is the enemy of meaning.
---
## The Deep Fear
Among the Duergar, there is a fear rarely spoken aloud.
That the stone is **remembering**.
That the pressure they revere is not passive, but patient.
That one day, something buried too deeply will decide it has endured long enough.
When the Duergar speak of this fear, they do not call it prophecy.
They call it **structural inevitability**.
