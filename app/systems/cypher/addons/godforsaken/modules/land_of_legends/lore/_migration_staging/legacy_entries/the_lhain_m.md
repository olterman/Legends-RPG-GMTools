## The Lhainîm
_The First Little Ones_
The **Lhainîm** are not a people who arrived.
They are a people who **remained**.
Before the shaping of forests and rivers, before names were given to beasts or stones, before even the Velim attended the thresholds, the Lhainîm were already present—woven into the land itself.
They are known by many names among mortals: **pixies, willows, pucks, nobs, redcaps**, and countless local titles now half-forgotten. To themselves, they are simply _Lhainîm_—_those who belong_.
---
## Nature Older Than Nature
The Lhainîm are not embodiments of nature as it is now, but of nature **as it was meant to be**—untamed, responsive, and alive with intention.
They do not guard forests.
They _are_ the forests remembering themselves.
Streams bend where Lhainîm pass. Stones shift their patience. Paths appear or vanish depending on old favors honored—or forgotten.
---
## Servants of the Elder Gods
Fellic myths and Velim silences agree on one point:
The Lhainîm once served the **Elder Gods**.
Not as worshippers, but as **helpers, attendants, and hands**—shaping growth, decay, and balance while greater forces moved beyond mortal perception.
When the Elder Gods withdrew, faded, or fell silent, the Lhainîm did not leave.
They stayed behind to tend what was unfinished.
This is why Lhainîm aid travelers, sabotage tyrants, bless fields, curse oath-breakers, and protect sacred places without pattern or mercy. They are following instructions **older than morality**.
---
## Many Forms, One Kin
The Lhainîm do not divide themselves by race or crown, but by **function and temperament**.
**Pixies** are quick spirits of air, light, and distraction—guides and misguides alike.
**Willows** are quiet watchers of groves, streams, and old boundaries, slow to anger and impossible to rush.
**Pucks** delight in disruption, teaching lessons through mischief and inversion.
**Nobs** are household spirits, guardians of hearth, tool, and threshold, loyal but demanding.
**Redcaps** are wardens of blood-soaked ground and broken oaths, feared even by other Lhainîm.
Despite their differences, they recognize one another instantly—and stand united when the land itself is threatened.
---
## The Lhainîm and the Other Peoples
**The Galadhrim** treat the Lhainîm with reverence and caution, knowing old pacts still bind them.
**The Fellic** acknowledge them as elders of instinct and memory.
**The Velim** tolerate them, recognizing their role predates gates and thresholds.
**The Alfirin** are deeply unsettling to the Lhainîm—too permanent, too bright, too resistant to correction.
**Humans** often mistake Lhainîm aid for luck—and their wrath for superstition.
The Lhainîm do not seek recognition.
They seek **continuation**.
---
## A Warning Told to Children
Among Fenmir, Galadhrim, and Fellic alike, an old saying persists:
> _Feed the small hands._
> _Mind the old paths._
> _Thank what you cannot see._
> _For the land remembers its servants._

---
## What the Lhainîm Represent
If the Velim maintain reality,
and the Fellic observe it,
and the elder races shape it—
Then the Lhainîm are what **keeps it alive**.
Not gentle.
Not fair.
But faithful to a world older than history.
